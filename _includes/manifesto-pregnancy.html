  • For solid foods: Solid Starts App and Website founded by Jenny Best - You can find a lot of content for free on their Instagram @solidstarts.
    • As of this writing, they offer their "first foods" database totally for free--with info on how to cut foods for babies dependingon their development stage and age: First Foods Database

Intro

I'm currently 39 weeks pregnant with my third (and last) baby. When I chat with folks at work or at summer camp pickup, other moms usually end up asking: "So, are you going with an epidural?" or while enroute to sharing a story or other advice, they'll pause to confirm: "You're doing an epidural, right?"

When I tell people that both of my births were unmedicated, sometimes I'll get more questions--usually about what I did to prepare and what pain coping mechanims I used. I always promise that I'll send them my labor playlist and specific videos that helped me, but half the time I never do, because I'm not sure how interested they actually are, or if they are just asking to be nice. :) So! I'm plopping the info right here for simple sharing and you can reach out to learn more--I'm an open book on this subject.

Warning: Some folks (coworkers? potential employers?) will look at this post and deem it totally inappropriate to share within the same space as my resume. If you think that might be you, please exercise your own free will and return Home. I promise you won't miss anything!

About my pregnancy and birthing experience: Slight anemia in early pregnancy, induced at 41wks, vaginal birth, no epidural.

TTC

Some things that were top of mind for us while TTC:
  • Take prenatals: There are tons in the market and not one seems to be perfect. I used Actif Organics in the past, which seemed... fine? But after realizing there's no real information about the company that makes them, I switched to Megafoods Baby & Me--and then realized they were on the higher end of the spectrum for heavy metals, so I switched to Mary Ruth's Liquid Prenatal and Postnatal, which was deemed decently low-heavy-metals in this source from Lead Safe Mama. I picked Mary Ruth's even though it wasn't lowest in the list because they use beta carotene instead of retinyl palmitate--to avoid any possibility of Vitamin A toxicity, which can cause all sorts of birth defects(??).
  • Exercise: Your good blood circulation is good for theirs. Exercise was hard for me, and I'd often let perfect be enemy of the good. What worked for me (in terms of willpower) was light hiking, speedwalking at the mall, lunges, some yoga, and always taking the stairs. I was doing fitness boxing twice a week when I found out I was pregnant with my second, and then my activity dwindled after that--I think more excercise would've helped me with labor with my second born. My husband ramped up exercise during this period, too.
  • Water: People warn you about having too much water, but you'd have to drink gallons a day for it to be considered dangerous. Make sure you drink a minimum 8 glasses, take more if you need and relax if you don't. I bought a 40oz jug and made sure to drink two fillups per day, starting from the TTC phase.
  • Stress: Avoid it. I felt stress differently during pregnancy--it felt more heinous. I did extra things to avoid stress (like avoid talking to certain people, avoid certain topics of conversations).
  • Alcohol and Diet: I don't drink alcohol (low tolerance), so this wasn't a problem for me, but my husband cut his usual 2-3 drinks per week down to 0 during this period of time. There are studies behind this that made this feel worthwhile. Other than that, I tried to eat slightly more healthy than usual, avoided sources of heavy metals (like tuna sushi), and started cutting down slightly on my caffeine.

Pregnancy

Cool, TTC is over! Hubby went back to enjoying alcoholic beverages while I quit coffee cold turkey and started to crave sushi, See's, and deli sandwiches. More on what was top of mind for me while pregnant:

  • Continued doing all the things I did while TTC: Drank two full jugs of my 40oz Hydroflask each day, tried to keep active, and took those prenatals
  • Intent and woo-woo stuff! I have reasons to foolishly believe that moms transfer some of their own thoughts and feelings onto their babies, while they're in the womb. Whether it's just cortisol or what, idk. During pregnancy, I try to use my brain as much as possible and operate under the assumption that I'm transferring some of my thinking and experiences onto my baby (mostly my thoughts and attitudes about situations and other people). Sorry, I warned you this would be woo-woo.

    Why do I think this?: I know this sounds crazy, but those little beans share so much with you while you are pregnant (you swap cells!), and stuff like breastmilk is so bizarrely magical and beyond current scientific comprehension--my thought is that when it comes to magical thinking and pregnancy, it's better to be safe than sorry and just assume that while pregnant, you transfer so much more than just oxygen and nutrients. This means I tried to exercise my brain and think critically, read a lot, thought kind and loving thoughts, and tried to stress less---you know, all the things I find hard to do when it's just me.

  • My favorite milestone: When they can start hearing you! 👂 Starting from 18 weeks, fetuses can start hearing stuff! I tried to read aloud, sing, and talk a lot throughout this second half of pregnancy. I also tried to have everyone in the family get close and talk to the baby too, so they'd recognize everyone's voices when they're born.
  • Older kids at home? One of my greatest fears is sibling jealousy, rivalry, and over-dependency. To try to help mitigate this, I start reading books about being an older sibling to get the other siblings hyped. When the baby comes, I make every effort to let my older kids gently interact with the baby as much as they want (and give them jobs to help with the baby!) so they feel included, wanted, and just as important. Of course, common sense (washed hands, etc) will be exercised, but basically I don't want the new baby to feel off-limits or more precious than the older kids are, and for me, this prep begins while I'm still pregnant.
  • Kegels and pelvic floor strengthening: Do your kegels! I forgot to do them with my second pregnancy and ended up having zero strength to push (how that baby came out, I do not know).

Labor & Delivery

Alrighy, it's go time:

  • A mantra that helped me with my second, that I'm already thinking about with my third:

    Birthing is the most natural thing in the world. Labor preps your uterus to eject your baby quickly and safely. Don't fight the progression--help your body do what it needs to do.

    This mindset was my main coping mechanism for my second birth.

  • I was woefully unprepared for my first. I watched all sorts of youtube videos, but watching videos isn't enough prep. I'll spare you the details so you don't go googling what things my induction entailed. :) I lucked out when a magical traveling nurse appeared just at the end of active labor. Sandra H, you are an angel. If she hadn't shown up, I would have wished I'd hired a doula.

If I can say one more woo-woo thing, it'd be that I think this is why my firstborn has much greater separation anxiety than my secondborn. I also think this is also why my firstborn couldn't pass meconium until day 3 of our stay at the hospital. I wish one of the nurses had told me that it was natural and safe to hold my baby as much as I wanted--I didn't hold her as much because she seemed so delicate and I was afraid I'd break her.

Breastfeeding

Thanks to Covid-era remote work & relaxed hybrid policies, I was able to exclusively breastfeed for 15 months with both babies.

  • The first couple days after birth, you won't have milk. You'll have colostrum--a clearish, protein-rich substance. Baby's belly is the size of a marble, so they will need to feed often while you make tiny amounts of colostrum for them.
  • Firstborn journey: The hospital made me supplement with formula for my first, and I wish I'd declined; I think it made it much harder to produce milk (mine came in on day 5 or 6--I almost gave up). That first week was insanely painful--it felt like I was extracting glass out of my nipples. (I know I did everything right, and latch was perfect--sometimes it just be like that. They never tell that you sometimes it just be like that.) Maybe there are products out there that help mitigate the pain for first-time breastfeeders. I didn't even think to look it up; I was out of mind in pain. After that first ~10 days, the whole experience felt pain-free and normal--there was a brief period a few months in where I was overproducing for roughly one week and it felt like I was literally boiling--cooling pads were great for this.
  • Secondborn journey: Zero issues. The body just knew what to do this time.

Maternity Leave (0-4 months)

  • Firstborn: I took all my leave at once. I thought I regretted not splitting it up, but then--
  • Secondborn: I split up my leave and hugely regretted it. It was more load to deal with all the backup coverage stuff twice. I'd recommend doing it in one fell swoop IF THAT'S WHAT YOU DESIRE. In fact, if you have the luxury, do exactly what you want and don't worry about what work things at all. Just be sure you discuss and communicate everything in advance with your boss so that everyone is clear on expectations and no one is surprised by anything.
  • Thirdborn: I've finally achieved my dream job, so I'm splitting it up--taking the six weeks of disability and will take the bonding time later, around holidays I think.
