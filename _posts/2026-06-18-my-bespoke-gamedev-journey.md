An executive producer and avid mentor at my company, when asked, "people are constantly reaching out to me asking how I got into the games industry--what do I tell them?"--wisely advised that when the volume of questions becomes unmanageable, to write a blog entry about it and start pointing folks there as the first stop. 

I've been meaning to do this very thing for a long time, and rather than put it off further, I'm just going to do it now on this, the evening before a holiday, after working till 10:44pm doing work I love. With two kids and a 37 week old baby gestating in my belly. I'm in the honeymoon phase of my dream job, so I figure there's no better time to write this post. 

Here goes! 


## First, so you know what drives me, here's a stream of consciousness, non-parallel bulleted list of what love about the game industry when it's at its best

* In a healthy studio, everyone is there for the same reason: **the opportunity to use the extent of our unique talents to work on something worthwhile**. To work unmolested by excessive bullshit. To create something bigger and better than we can achieve individually. Making it all come together somehow.
* Being around crazy smart and creative people who are deeply, deeply passionate about what they do. 
* Art, problem solving, and creativity. All the time.
* Learn something new every day. ... Every moment sometimes.
* We all believe games are good and important. 
* We love artists and people who create things. 
* Every artist I've ever met is incurably curious and passionate about something. They constantly want to be better at their thing than they were yesterday. They are so inspiring. 
* Watching artists and designers work makes me feel alive and human. 

So that's the TL;DR of why this industry, for me. 

## Now, here's what I hate about the industry when it's at its worst

* Too many people from tech who are only here for the prestige and money. 
* Too many people who are here but don't respect the creatives/artists/designers/content creators.  
* Did I mention all the tech people? When I look around and find more than half the folks around are ex-Google, ex-Amazon, it makes me very afraid. They walk in and often all they can see is all the inefficiencies--not the exploration that happens underneath beautiful outcomes, but just countable inputs and outputs. 

## Here's where the adventure forks

If the "what drives me" block of text bores you and the "worst" block of text desribes you, then: 
* I recommend reading something like Blood, Sweat, and Pixels by Jason Schreier to see if that appeals to you. 
* Also, it sounds like you get bored very easily--if that's the case, I don't think the games industry is for you right now. Be interested in life! Find the thing that excites you and spend time doing it--I believe in you! 
* If you're unwilling to do that, or that book bores you, then I recommend you follow gamedev influencers on LinkedIn instead of reaching out to me; I'm simply not the droid you're looking for. 

If the "what drives me" block of text resonates and you've "GOTTA GET IN THERE"... I still don't know if I can help you, but you're certainly welcome to read on and ask me followup questions if you have any. 

## Okay, here we go for real then

I just want to say upfront: I'm sorry but I can't give you a referral. 

The industry is rough right now and I have too many excellent former colleagues who've been out of work for years to put a random acquaintence above them with any sort of referral. Hopefully by the time you finish reading this, you will still feel motivated and undeterred from pursuing this on your own if that's your dream. 

## Life story

When I was 9, I started a short-lived neighborhood newsletter with my best friend, which ran for maybe two issues and were delivered to mailboxes in my cul-de-sac. Then, we tried to start a dog biscuit business... we made something cluelessly without a recipe and quit after our first sale because it didn't feel right. 

I got my first job selling tshirts at Pike Place Market in Seattle when I was 16, then worked at a teriyaki shop for the rest of high school, got an apprenticeship at the Museum of Flight and won their Eastman scholoarship award, did a lot of writing and English tutoring, and then went on to college. I worked throughout college (sandwich shop, retail, tutoring, newspaper writing, volunteering in web development, internships etc) while double-degreeing in Technical Communication and Psychology. I took 18 credits every quarter at the University of Washington (because it was the same cost as 15 credits). It took me 5 years to do undergrad because of the double-degree and a 6-month full-time internship at IBM's Silicon Valley Labs. 

I graduated just after the housing market crisis of 2008. My dad told me to brace myself--it would probably be impossible for me to get a job in my field of study because of it. But I was insanely lucky at the time and my internship boss told me that I got the last hiring "chip" that the department had to offer. I got to be a technical writer straight out of college. In my dream city: Boston. (Well--Littleton, MA.) 

## Built different

Why did I tell you that? 

Because the game industry is full of insanely driven people like me. I think this is what you it takes to succeed as an outsider. A shit ton of hard work and strategic positioning. I did the sandwich job because I wanted to experience working in a kitchen. I did the newspaper writing because doing so made me a university employee and waived some of my tuition. I did the retail job at Limited Too because I was obsessed with Lizzie McGuire. (Okay, maybe ignore that last one. Worst job ever, btw.) 

I am not special--everyone in games has a story that is entirely different from mine but is similarly full of drive. I think what makes us alike is that we: 

* Take responsibility for our actions and don't blame. 
* We solve problems. 
* We fill our lives with interesting experiences, which tends to open doors and broaden one's thinking. Mine just happened to be work, work, and work. 

## First foot in the door

When I started at IBM, my secret goal was to be the best technical writer in the world. 

I wanted to be able to explain things more clearly and intuitively than anyone else. 

After 8 years at IBM, I was laid off in 2016. At around that time, I met my future husband. While I was applying for jobs and working on my new portfolio site, he got me into games--mostly Magic the Gathering and League of Legends--and it dawned on me that there were people out there who worked on games for a living. The thought had never crossed my mind until my layoff. I decided then that Magic the Gathering cards were the most beautiful things I ever saw. I'm still pretty bad at the game, but the information on the cards, the art, and flavor are just beautiful to me. 

Before that point, the only game I'd played a lot of as a kid was Chip's Challenge--a top-down tile-based puzzle game that I probably played about 200 hours of. The most difficult levels were so insane that you just had to choreograph your finger placements on the keyboard to specific timings. I loved it. My parents didn't want us to get addicted to games so we didn't have any video games at home and I grew up moving constantly so I had no friends to show me that games even existed. That is all to say--I am a late-bloomer in terms of gaming. 

I figured that game studios might need technical writers. So I kept an eye out for a role like that for about a year until one appeared for Infinity Ward, in the exact office park that my boyfriend (same one--the future husband) was working in. We joked that we should go for it just so we could have our lunches together, mere buildings away from one another. It would be so convenient! 

I wrote a killer cover letter that was probably tinged a little bit like this blog post. It was honest, confident, explained my obsessive desire to write procedures clearly and intuitively, and I got my interview. I remember that they were especially impressed by the IBM experience. 

The interview was an in-person group setting at their office. The office took my breath away... it was so cool and stylish. I remember that when I sat down with the team I'd be working with, I felt like I'd come home somehow. They were all curious, inventive, and full of creativity. They each wanted to be more and more exceptional at their thing. I could feel a sort of obsessive nerdiness that I didn't know others possessed (again, I just didn't have exposure to people in general--I was painfully shy, a complete loner in high school and most of college). 

Anyway, I figured that my best shot at the interview would be to knock their socks off about everything I believed in as a technical writer. How to manage stakeholders and SMEs and the review process, how to get buy-in from users, user testing my documentation, doing contextual inquiry and requirements gathering, etc. 

I got the job! It was for a 12-month contract but I didn't care. I was happy it wasn't permanent because I thought the worst thing would be if I couldn't meet the challenge and they would be *stuck* with me until I got PIP'ed out or left to preserve my honor or whatever. (I really thought I would fail at this. These people looked very smart and very confident.)

## Number go up

I set out to prove myself as immediately as possible. Don't believe it when they tell you that you can take a month to ramp up. Deliver or prove value on week 1 if you can.
